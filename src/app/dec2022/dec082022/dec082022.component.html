Introduction

An excellent software project starts with a simple idea. It's the idea that comes from people who love using technology and want to make their lives easier and better. An example of this type of inspiration is the first time someone uses Google Maps and realizes it can tell them where to go—no more guessing when driving directions are involved. That's a huge deal. But if you're like me, you might have noticed that some companies consistently produce products that inspire excitement in users while others struggle or just don't get it right. What makes these two scenarios different? In my experience working on software projects over the past decade or so, I've learned there are certain things we can do as individuals or teams that make one type more likely than another:

You can't build the next big hit by yourself

Indeed, you need help to build the next big hit. You will need to hire people with unique skillsets and experience who bring different interests and tastes and have different backgrounds, perspectives, and opinions.

Hiring is one of the most crucial aspects of starting a successful software project because your team needs to work together seamlessly for years on end for it all to come together. The first person you hire sets the tone for how your entire organization will be run—and if they aren't a good fit from day one? Then it's going to be pretty hard for things to get off the ground (and stay there).

Build a diverse team

A diverse team is more likely to be creative and innovative. Research shows that this isn't just because of the variety of perspectives they bring—it's also because they talk more with each other. This fosters cooperation, trust, and understanding, leading to better problem-solving. A diverse team communicates more effectively as there are fewer assumptions about what "everyone knows" or how things should be done (because everyone does not know everything).

There's no replacement for working directly with users

The best way to design products that people love is to get feedback directly from users. But if you're unsure how to do that or are afraid of what they might say, fear not. We've been there and have a few tips on making the most of your user research efforts.

Make sure you're talking with the right people

Don't ask people what they want - ask them what's important for your product. Ask open-ended questions and let the conversation guide itself.

Software is never truly finished

The best you can do is to ship it, but even then, it will never be finished. Software is a process, not a product. It's an ever-changing collection of code that adapts to new features or technologies over time.

Software is truly finished once it's no longer needed by your customers or becomes obsolete due to changes in technology or industry standards (or if you stop paying your hosting bill).

If the people who use your software are not smiling, you're doing it wrong. There are two key measures for success in a software project:

  • Are the users happy with your software?
  • If not, what can you do to make them happy?

Make sure you always listen to users and try to improve. Although it's tempting to think of users as the people who are supposed to be excited about your product, they're also the ones who can tell you precisely what they want and don't want in a product. So, don't just try and sell them what you think is best—listen to them. If you're not sure how well something is working, put it out there and watch what happens. If a user has a problem using your product, take note: You can either fix whatever issue or see if something bigger is going on here that affects all of your users.

Keep an open mind; if someone tells you their idea for an app would be better than yours (or even just different), give it some serious consideration before shutting down the conversation entirely. Maybe something about their concept will enrich or improve how yours works; perhaps it won't work at all, but even still—just because one person thinks something isn't worth pursuing doesn't mean nobody else will either!

Remember to try new things! Even though one idea didn't pan out, other options are available; keep brainstorming until one stick (and keep improving on those ideas). Be willing to change course if necessary—even after launch day! The only way people will truly love using your software is if they feel like they have input into its development process too--not just during beta testing but also after release, which means listening attentively every step along the way.

Conclusion

We hope you've found this guide on how to start a software project that inspires excitement useful. We know it can be intimidating to start something new, but we promise it gets easier as time goes on! With these tips and tricks in mind (and maybe a little inspiration), you're one step closer to creating the next big hit. Good luck :)
